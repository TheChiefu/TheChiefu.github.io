Preface

To preface this review I must stress that it is written from a visual learner’s perspective and will have bias in that regard due to the nature of how I learn. If you aren’t too visually inclined this information may still be valuable but make sure to remember this bias when reading. With that out of the way, let’s get started!

Kanji, it’s every Japanese learner’s biggest fear when first coming across the language. If you’re a stubborn/slow learner like me it can seem like this impossible feat to be able to read anything other than 平仮名（ひらがな）and 片仮名「カタカナ」since it’s already 46 characters to learn for each syllabary. However Wanikani takes the approach of using a “Spaced Repetition System” or better known as “SRS” [1] [2] on top of “Mnemonics” to make learning Kanji more digestible. Before going into Wanikani itself let’s understand exactly the underlying systems at play what make it (at least for me) a very powerful tool.

The Psychology Behind Wanikani

To start of, let’s talk about SRS. It’s the idea of using reviewing concepts within certain time frames to cement them in your brain’s long term memory. So you start by learning a concept for the first time, so you learn it as best as you can, then recall it a short time later. Once you feel confident in your ability to remember that concept, you wait a certain interval of time (let’s say an hour), then review it again. If you recall it without issue you exponentially increase the time of review until you become proficient at recalling a concept.

So for example if we want to learn the phrase for “Banana Bread” in French we look up or find the phrase: “le pain de banane”[3]. We then learn it to the best we can, then we recall it in 10 minutes. If we recall the phrase we successfully have remembered it in our short term memory. Now we recall it in an hour, if successful move it to 1 day, then 3 days, ad nauseam (or until a year passed, then you’re probably good). This concept can apply to anything it doesn’t have to language learning, but Wanikani and other language apps use this system for recall to help learners pick up a language. Now that we’ve covered SRS let’s talk about “Mnemonics”!

In essence “Mnemonics” is a visual learning way to remember concepts. However unlike just visual learning it adds another layer to the visual stimuli. It uses stories and other lingual concepts to append to the concept you’re learning. So for example when I had to learn the 46 Hiragana, I imagined them as pictograms that look like certain objects. So when I first learned 「し」 I imagined someone holding a “SHIeld” (emphasis on the “shi” part since it creates the same sound as the symbol) to remember that that symbol/concept. Again with 「つ」being a “Tsunami” wave, 「え」being an ice skater, 「ぬ」being “Noodles”, and so on. It doesn’t exclusively have to be similar sounding to English like I did with 「し、つ、ぬ」, as long as there is a conceptual relation between two ideas that make the new concept stick better. Now enough for the psychology lessons on learning concepts, now to the actual tool at hand!

What is Wanikani

Wanikani is a site that uses both the concepts of SRS and Mnemonics as it’s foundations, making the content structured based on the assumption that the system they’ve created will know the best time for you to review. They’ve laid the content in Levels with easier content at level 1 and the hardest content at level 60. Meaning if you’re starting from zero getting into Wanikani can be great since it’s a structured path laid out for you.

If you have some experience in Japanese it may be tricky if you want to learn specific Kanji at your own pace because you have to learn the earlier concepts first before being able to move on to the content you want to use. However either way if you’re interested in using the service there is an underlying assumption that you already know both Hiragana and Katakana. This is because in order to learn Kanji and it’s related concepts, you need to know at least Hiragana to be able to read the Kanji and Vocabulary. This is crucial since there are 3 main categories of terms that Wanikani presents to you: Radicals, Kanji, and Vocabulary.

Radicals are technically a real “things” that Kanji are made up of (科 – “Course” being made up of both: 禾 [grain] and 斗 [ladle] ) but Wanikani often has their own meanings and radicals so I am most critical of this aspect of the website. The concept makes sense because more complicated Kanji like (警, 薬, 識) are made of other radicals, however I personally believe that if you’re learning Kanji in the first place you’re learning the easier Kanji first then using that knowledge to build up and learn the composite Kanji not learning radicals and Kanji as independent concepts. To my destain when using the website Kanji you already learned appear as radicals and I just see it as pointless padding. However enough babbling about radicals let’s get to what we’re really here for Kanji!

Kanji are the big scary Chinese symbols that make up the bulk of Japanese text. In Wanikani you start out with the basics like: (一、二、三、四、etc) and build you’re way up to fun ones like: (鬱、翻、鰐). Don’t worry about how scary the last 3 are, if you know the basics learning complex Kanji can actually be pretty straight forward since they are composed of other Kanji. Wanikani has you learn either the 訓読み (Kun’yomi or Japanese Reading) or the 音読み (On’yomi or Chinese reading) to start off and once you get them right enough times you are then given Vocabulary terms using the Kanji you have proven to memorize.

Vocabulary is the most straight forwards since it’s applicable in all languages. Wanikani is pretty much just like a normal flash card tool when it comes to Vocabulary since it’s just memorizing words like you would any language. The reason they put them after the Radical and Kanji is that once you’ve memorized the level’s previous concepts you can use that knowledge to learn words quicker. Since there’s been times when I’m learning a new word and before I click on the “next” button to see the reading I can most likely guess it because I know the vocabulary word’s Kanji readings. But that should cover the over-arching topics that make up Wanikani, let’s now talk about the elephant in the room; the pricing.

User Experience

When first using Wanikani you should be loaded in to your “Dashboard” where you’ll see available “Lessons”, “Reviews”, “Review Forecast”, and a plethora of menus. Whenever you have anything new to learn the “Lessons” section should be a pink color with the number of new concepts to learn. Similarly the “Reviews” section will show any reviews you have available accompanied with a blue background and image.

Below the Lessons and Reviews section should be your progress meter, this changes depending on what you’re currently learning. In the image provided above my progress is tracking my amount of “Kanji passed” before I can “level up” to learn vocabulary words and eventually a new level. Below that meter is the level’s items such as: Radicals and Kanji. Here you can track how much progress you’ve made on individual item’s progress. Finally to the right is the “Review Forecast” where you can check at what times new reviews will appear.

Below all of the level specific information you can see your overall account progress / statistics. The five boxes that are labeled at the top of the image above show how many items are at an “item’s individual level” or officially the “SRS Stage”. Meaning that if an item is considered an “Apprentice Level” item it will show up in reviews often until it is pushed to a “guru level”, then “master level”, so on a so forth if you keep getting it right. If you at any point get an item wrong it pushes it down a level and will have to re-learn it from that level again. To see a more detailed break down of the SRS Stages click the provided link.

Finally bellow the SRS Stages is the general information column sections. The leftmost showing you the newest unlocks you’ve made in the last 30 days, the center showing you “Critical Condition Items” or items you’ve been consistently been getting wrong (also providing a “correct” percentage rate), and the rightmost showing items you’ve most recently “burned” or “memorized”. That should cover the Dashboard now to cover Reviews and Lessons.

Reviews & Lessons

To start off let’s start off with Lessons

Lessons are as their title implies, a session in which you can learn new concepts with provided information. They are split into different sections depending on what you are learning. The sections are as follows:

  • Radicals
    • Name (Containing: Mnemonic)
  • Kanji
    • Meaning (Containing: Mnemonic & English Translation of Meaning)
    • Readings (Containing: Either On’yomi or Kun’yomi)
  • Vocabulary
    • Kanji Composition (How word is composed of Kanji)
    • Meaning (Containing: Mnemonic)
    • Reading (Hiragana reading of word)
    • Context (Example sentences)

The Meaning section is where Wanikani provides one of it’s primary selling points, mnemonics.

Usually within the “Explanation” section is a story that the creators of the website have created to help you memorize concepts. What this is trying to accomplish is create imagery in which your brain uses to latch on to a hard concept to make remembering it easier. Since if a story is absurd the more likely you are to memorize it. However over time as you recall the concept and get it right the story is supposed to drop from memory as the concept’s meaning stays, utilizing the psychology of SRS.

This section is incredibly helpful especially in the early stages since Kanji/Radicals are pictographs and connecting concepts to them help their meanings stick easier; so pay attention to these if you are having trouble with them. If you are having no trouble understanding them and are getting them correct without needing mnemonics then feel free to skip theses sections.

Once you’re done with a Lesson you’ll move onto the Review section.

Here you’ll be provided with a screen in which you’ll be asked for an item’s English meaning (Radical, Kanji, Vocabulary), a Kanji’s On’yomi or Kun’yomi reading, or a Vocabulary term’s reading. If you get the item correct your text box will glow up with green, if you get it wrong it will turn red and you can review an item’s details by pressing the “Item Info” button in the bottom row.

Here details of an item are explained to you, in this example above it’ll show the reading since that’s the item being quizzed. If I were answering the vocabulary meaning it would show that instead. However you can view all of an item’s details by clicking on the “Show All Information” button at the bottom. Once you are done you’ll be presented with the “Summary” screen showing you what items you’ve just reviewed.

The only difference between Lessons and Reviews is the fact that reviews just review terms you’ve already learned and use the same user interface as the second half of lessons. Useful Extras

If you ever want to look at any of the radical, kanji, or vocabulary items in detail you can click on the respective tab drop-downs on the top of your dashboard page. Such as “Levels”, “Radicals”, “Kanji”, “Vocabulary” or using the search bar. Each drop-down menu provides a list of items per 10 levels and per section a breakdown of the items as individual items. This is useful if you want to look into each item on a more specific basis.

To access more advanced features you can click on your profile in the top right corner of the Dashboard and a drop-down menu of options should appear.

  • Profile: Brings you to a page displaying your personal statistics and overall progress in Wanikani.
  • Settings
    • App: Adjust WK settings such as audio auto-playing in reviews, number of new words per lesson, etc
    • Account: Where you can change your username, password, and other account details
    • API Tokens: Where you can generate API tokens that let other services use your WK account
    • Profile: Where you can create a bio and share link social links to your profile
    • Danger Zone: Where you can reset level progress or delete your account
  • Subscription: Allows you to adjust your current subscription settings.
  • Sign Out: Signs you out
  • Community: Brings you to the Wanikani community forms were you can interact with other Wanikani users

There also exists a help page if you have any more detailed questions.

Conclusion

Wanikani can be an incredibly powerful tool if you stick to it and study everyday. I know when I started in Japanese 101 in my University it helped get me jump started on being able to read since instead of rote-memorization like I learned in school I found that the stories made the Kanji stick for me. I was initially scared of taking the plunge into Kanji since it’s overwhelming when you first look at it but Wanikani breaks it down into manageable levels that mask the amount of Kanji there is.

I personally lucked out on that aspect of using the tool at the right time since I was learning Kanji from scratch at school and on Wanikani so I personally don’t know the experience of jumping in after having previous knowledge on Kanji. However I will say that even if you’ve studied using Kanji in compound words without knowing the exact meaning behind them I will stress that (at least for me) knowing what those characters mean helps me learn new words faster and understand old words better because I have a better understanding of what it means. Instead of memorizing a big concept like 地理的特徴 as “Geographical Features” I can break it down into:

  • 「地理」（ちり）Geography or (Ground 地 + Reason 理)
  • 「的」（てき）Like (form adjective from nouns)
  • 「特徴」（とくちょう）Features/Trait or (Special 特 + Indication/Sign 徴)

Taking each Kanji meaning into consideration and understanding how that word is composed. Obviously when reading I’m not breaking it down that much but to get the fundamental understanding down I do, then as I come across it more often, the better it sticks. It’d be absurd if I remembered it as “Ground reason-ish special indication”, but it sure helps build up the image in my head.

So having a website that teaches mean what all these symbols mean, how to read them, how to apply them to words, etc to me is worth the value the service asks for. I intend to learn Japanese for a long time and I intend to master it to the best of my ability so for someone like me buying a lifetime is worth it’s weight in gold. However if you’re just getting into learning Kanji take the chance for the first 3 levels if you think this approach is a good fit. If you don’t like it you don’t lose anything and if you do you have a website with a plethora of information and a helpful community.

I just have to warn you however if you do start to keep up on on the reviews daily since it’s slooooooow in the beginning. So keep reviewing to get some items into rotation so that whenever you check the website you have something to do rather than no review or lessons when you’re itching to learn.

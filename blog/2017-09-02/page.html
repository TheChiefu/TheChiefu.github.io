The Development of Reclamation

Written: 2017-09-02

​My first rodeo in Game Design

​Reclamation was a project of inspiration, hope, and regret. Why regret? Well many reasons can contribute to my feeling of regret on the project but lets focus on what happened in the development of the project first.

It was a new semester in my final year of public schooling, my Computer Technology & Game Design class had been pretty stagnant for the 2 & 1/2 years I’d been there and all we essentially did there was copy code from what our teacher gave us into a Dark Basic Pro; a really old game engine without outdated architecture. One of my friends even considered dropping the class because it had been so stagnant; but then a ray of hope came down, our new computer tech teacher had finally been brought in. I really didn’t know what to expect at first honestly, after being in such a drab class for so long I kept skeptical.

It was until he sat us down and asked us what WE wanted to do instead of assigning us busy work that I started to enjoy the class. Before we even started the game he had us sit down and watch the movie “The Wizard”, mostly because it reminded him of our childhood but it gave us a breather from the previously mundane tasks of the class of old. His big lesson before letting us roam free with the possibility of creating a game idea was to emphasize the importance of gameplay over graphics, an ideology I agree with completely. We were given a good week of relaxing time due to the mid-terms in the rest of our classes, then we all sat together to come up with a game-plan on what to do for the rest of the year.

​He suggested we come up with a game concept that we can present to the director of the Career and Technical center of our school to show off how the class had improved from how it used to be run. The first thing we did was plan out when certain steps had to be completed: step one was conceptualization, step two development, and finally step three presentation. We had a date when we had to present the concept of our game to the director and we started on working. Not after a hilarious naming for our “company”, which was a joke created by one of our team-mates. The name was “Lardhard”, which came from the polar opposite of the 10th grader’s “company” name of “Oceansoft”; we laughed the joke off at first but since nobody could agree on a name we kept the name “Landhard”.

Most of the team was directed to work on creating artwork for the game, such as creature drawings, world map concepts, and the like. Two of our members created music and sound for the game, one focusing on music and the other sound effects. One person was supposed to be the project lead but it ended being about 2/3 people when certain people were absent. Others were tasked with creating how the control scheme would be if it were released, some creating gameplay mechanic ideas, and then I was tasked with creating an interactive demo from pulling in all the ideas from the team into the project.

Although all we had to do was create essentially PowerPoint of ideas and present them to one of our administrators at school, I really wanted to force myself to really learn game design and learn the inner-workings of a game engine. So even though at the time it felt fun and often times frustrating it really helped me out in the long-run. I learned how to implement UI sliders from Unity in (I know ​​​advanced​ right?), change weather controls which could be easily implemented in random weather conditions, natural time-of-day progression, etc. I’ve always wanted to create an open world Zelda type game ever since I was in Elementary school (kind of like Breath of the Wild before it came out) and this was my opportunity to learn a bit on how to design one. If I ever find my original designs I’d gladly share them here.

​However, once the time came we scrambled together a Google Slides and presented it to the director of the technical center and she loved it, since before the class was literally Photoshop your face on a celebrity and create pong. She loved the connection of artistic ideas with technical skills in order to create art. Okay enough for the back-padding… This was essentially the practice run for the real presentation at the end of the year. It started out as a simple presentation to the staff but it gradually turned into the local news showing up, and the superintendent were going to be there.

I gradually got more agitated however, not only because I felt like the pressure was on but that we’re not going to make an actual game rather a concept. Granted I still enjoyed creating the landscape and concept of the game but I didn’t want to falsely excite people into creating the next Skyrim. It was mostly because I knew the pressure would be on me and a friend because we’re really the only ones how now how to code and partly 3D model. I don’t want to bad-mouth my friend/peers, I loved the idea we created but I didn’t have the confidence that we’d be able to actually create the grand-idea for the game; creating a game idea is easy but putting one together is a different beast all on its own.

​Weeks past of development and the days were counting down, the project was nearing completion and we had to focus on getting all of us together to write a presentation script and slideshow. Most people shifted focus to writing their pieces of the presentation, starting with the story to art, music, gameplay mechanics, marketing, etc. While this was coming to fruition and I was hard-work finishing up the interactive demo for the presentation; I reworked it because there were tons of bugs that needed to be fixed. The demo was originally supposed to give people the ability to walk around the town or at least fly around but due to an error in Unity’s physics that I didn’t know how to fix would clip the only the player through the terrain. I then found out the presentation wouldn’t really allow us the time to have people “play” it so I created a multi-camera system in which the Alphanumeric keys changed the camera to static positions. In the end this was the better decision since it allowed for people to visualize the story since each camera angle had a connection to the story enhancing the experience.


Unity 3D: 2017-04-08

Finally the presentation day, everyone’s hard-work could finally come together and be presented to an audience. We had to scramble last minute to get everything to work properly, the projector used in our school’s library had a mind of its own and would work whenever it wanted to but we had luck on our side that day. I brought in my personal computer since non of the MacBook Airs the school had could run the game. I brought two monitors from our class room and hid them behind a presenter podium, both vertical. One mirroring the projector so I could see what the audience is seeing and the bottom off-screen to prepare music, script transitions, etc that I needed to know about without distracting the audience.

​We presented the game and the reception was phenomenal! I’m no social butterfly but I genuinely had a fun time, two strangers from the audience that seemed to be 2 years younger walked up to me and asked about the development process and actually had something to teach someone about, which I never do. In conclusion, although I dreaded some parts of the process I felt proud of my work since it taught me good lessons of game design, experience working with a team and deadline on when the game needed to be done, and most importantly something to work forward to in the future; a more grandiose picture of what games I want to make. That goal right now… is to make Taurus a reality and not just a homemade project to have fun with my friends.

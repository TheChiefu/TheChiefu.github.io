End-Year Update

Written: 2023-11-24

Settling in and Scanning Photos

After finally settling back at home and cleaning up my old room I started to get my daily route back in order. I used my work laptop to check emails everyday and join required meetings but knew that basically no work was being assigned anymore to people in AZ. So I mostly kept applying for jobs online and work on Taurus for fun. It was around this time the Unity Engine changes were popping up and I decided to drop work on Taurus in Unity in favor of Unreal and started a Udemy course there. I kept up with my Japanese but kept it to “reviews only” since I still felt stress in the whole process of moving and I didn’t want to overwhelm myself with tons of new words.

At some point when getting something to drink from the refrigerator I saw some family photos from downstairs sitting on the couch. I asked if it was any photos I scanned before from the last few times I visited and found out they weren’t. Little did I know this was a start to a new adventure I’d embark for a month or two. By this time it was early September and I started to research how to scan 35mm film and figure out a way to add them to the family google drive. Well, I got a dedicated machine for scanning and started my work; I got binders and sheets to store them properly and took time time meticulously scanning each film. I think it took 3 weeks alone of mostly scanning throughout the day to get everything done.

By this point I got the interest to just “get it all done” and ask for literally every photo, video, and tape we know of in the family to archive. I ended up getting an 8mm reel-to-reel machine to scan those tapes, a capture card to record old VHS tapes and etc. Towards the end of finishing those up I asked my uncle if he had anything left. My grandmother asked him and from what she said, his response was “he’ll regret asking”. A few days later I woke up and saw an 18 gallon tote, a big paper box, and stack of photos near the door.

At this point I knew I needed to revise my archiving solution. For the longest time I thought I already scanned the majority of the family photos and put them on google drive. Mainly the container they were from, so photos in “Blue Binder #1” where put in a folder of the same name online. However now that there were over 2000 photos, finding anything you actually wanted was a nightmare. So during my process of scanning I decided that I’ll plan on writing a PHP/MySQL site to manage it for me.

The idea is that “all I have to do” is make a landing page where people log into a website and search up the image they want. Instead of navigating through folders of random images on Google Drive. That brings us to where I am now; still scanning photos but nearing the end of the process.

I’ll admit I have gone completely overkill on the family archive since 99% of people wanting to look at the photos will just take a glance at the photos and move on. However from doing lots of research on file formats, encoding methods, archival methods, etc; I’ve learned how to do it the “proper way”. My reasoning being that, if I’m going to do this once I’ll do it the right way instead of fixing things down the line later and regretting doing it improperly.

What I mean by the “improper” way is how I had been archiving photos in the past. Which was scanning photos on a flatbed scanner, cropping them, editing them, etc then saving them to JPG at 60% quality. What this means is that if I find out later that the photo is reversed or something is incorrect I have to find the original document in-person and rescan it. The reason I can’t edit the JPG and flip the image is that every time you save a JPG it re-compresses the file; so multiple saves down the line it means it’ll get worse and worse.

What I’ve started doing now is storing two copies. An lossless original scan at the highest quality I can store at and a compressed / lossy one that will be viewable online. What this allows is that the “lower” quality web versions can be looked at and “criticized”. If an error is spotted I can pull up the original scan and export a new compressed version. I have yet to do this with the original documents I scanned years ago, but I plan to do those months later since I don’t want to drive myself insane… (well more insane).

The original reason why I only stored JPEG versions in the past was to make sure I wasn’t using too much space on Google Drive. But now that I plan on hosting the site, storage isn’t an issue anymore. I could keep rambling on about the photos but I’ll try to end this post with my future career and life prospects.

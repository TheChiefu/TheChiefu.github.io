Being Proactive

Written: 2023-02-16

Organizing My Media

What I mean by “organizing” my media collection is that I have an assortment of: Blu-rays, CDs, and other media on my devices that I keep for entertainment. However they’re either incomplete (songs without lyrics, artists, etc), too big (some 4K movies are 88GBs big), or unfound (obscure Japanese songs I can’t find the source to). So the goal there is to organize that data so that it’s in a place where I feel like it’s “finished” and organized.

Music

I guess a more concrete example of what I mean by organizing for example (that pertains to music) would be my old YouTube playlist backup. So back in 2014 or so I used to use YouTube for my music collection but noticed over time videos would get taken down. In 2017 I used a tool to convert all the videos to mp3 files and saved them to my phone as a backup so I’d never lose the songs. For a time this was fine, however as I got into CDs more over time I noticed how much better the CD quality sounded over YouTube videos.

This was because most YouTube videos (seem to) cap audio at 128kbps mp3 quality and CDs are uncompressed audio. In layman’s terminology, it’s like listening to someone talk on the phone vs in person (not as bad as a phone, but not too far off). Once I heard how much of a massive difference CD sounded over the YouTube songs I started an adventure in college to find the sources of my YouTube playlists over time. For more popular music like: The Beatles, Metallica, and the like it was easy; just type in the artist and album and buy the CD.

However, since all my backups were from YouTube all I had to go off of was the title of the video. Meaning if I wanted to find some obscure Japanese song or anime soundtrack and the video title didn’t have all the information there, I’d have no where to do. Doubly so if the original video was deleted and no alternative was uploaded. Triply (is that word?) so, since back then I didn’t know any Japanese so looking up obscure media was a shot in the dark.

What ended up happening was, I bought all the music I could find at stores, online, etc for as many songs as I could (I had like 3000 songs between all my playlists) and keeping the remaining unfound media as 128kbps mp3 files on my computer laying remnant. Over the years I slowly chipped away at some songs, but some seemed impossible to find; until at the beginning of this year I seemed to have made a breakthrough. Now that I crammed thousands of Japanese words into my head over the years the previously obscure media that I couldn’t find the sources to, I was now able to. I bought and imported music over the last couple weeks and have gotten it down my YouTube collection to 10 unknown songs (9 which have names but I can’t find a CD quality version anywhere but 1 with a name I can’t seem to match anywhere, it’s literally called “Touhou Song”).

While I search for those I also have been adding lyrics to every song in my collection (13,330 according to my phone; at least 40% are instrumentals), converting the meta-data on my Japanese albums to contain the Japanese original names and artists, and overall cleaning up everything. So instead of say a song being: “Hikouki Gumo by Yumi Arai” it says “ひこうき雲 – 荒井由実. Although it seems pointless (and truthfully it kind of is) I noticed over time it really helps me reinforce words I’ve learned over time. Especially when adding lyrics since there’s been a couple times where I had to transcribe lyrics and it gave me some listening practice.

TV and Movies

For this collection I don’t have much of a story like I do with my music, but still it’s something that bothered me for a bit so I decided to tackle it. Currently my TV shows are 90% English so organizing them isn’t too bad, however some of the anime I have is from US/UK releases so I had to do some work on them to get them “organized” to my liking.

I changed the names of each episode to the original Japanese ones and use what’s called an “OCR” or “Optical Character Recognition” program on the subtitles. On Blu-rays, subtitles are bitmap image files and the OCR takes those images and tries to figure out a text equivalent character of said file. When it’s done running I can use the extracted text (as .srt files) and add them back to the movie file. I did this since some devices don’t support certain subtitle files but support “srt” ones universally.

Additionally since some audio types are not supported on all devices (mainly my phone and tablet) such as Dolby Digital 7.1 audio, I use a combination of software (mainly ffmpeg) to convert the audio to FLAC files (which all devices can read) and recombine the audio back into the TV/movie file. I mainly do this for movies since most TV shows aren’t using surround sound, so it’s not as a monumental task as it seems.

As for movies I noticed that all my 4K HDR movies seemed to be around 60-80 GBs per movie and wanted to reduce those sizes for my portable devices. What I used to do when I traveled was copy 1-2 movies to my phone’s SD card and hope that’d be enough. However I got frustrated (especially doing long flights/days) since I’d run out of things to watch. So I thought of re-encoding videos to get smaller versions for my phone, but with HDR movies I noticed that the encoding would be off and never worked so I put it off.

However, recently with the AV1 encoder being more well supported now I’ve been able to reduce file sizes significantly. A 80GB movie could be compressed down to 8-12GBs with proper color mapping. Additionally, even when I try pixel peeping on my TV I couldn’t tell a difference between the source and compressed version. So I started to use my computer to run a few hours a day to get my movie collection down to a small enough size to put all on my small devices.

Concluding Thoughts on Media Organization

All of this is very unneeded and over the top, but I guess at some point I deemed it necessary for the sake of convenience (especially when traveling in terms of my movies). Also, since it was in the back of my head I deemed it necessary to figure out. I’m just glad I decided to take on this endeavor now instead of leaving it since I know in the back of my mind there was still old songs I haven’t listened to in years I wanted to find the sources of and wanted to put more media on my mobile devices without swapping SD cards.

Also at this point with how many hours I have put into researching, editing, and organizing my media (especially music) collection it’s kind of become apart of personal journey for me. It’s not just songs I like but a “thing” that I emotionally tie to parts of my life. Especially songs that I first listened to years ago and then found the sources to. Going from YouTube quality to full surround in some songs (especially obscure anime songs) was an emotional experience.

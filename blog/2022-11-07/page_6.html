Half Western and Half Eastern

Written: 2022-11-07

Influence of Anime

It can’t be denied anime has had a big influence on many people around the world and yes some people idealize Japan though anime, but for me I never used it in such a way. Similar to video games I always saw it as a way to immerse myself in another world and experience whatever emotion(s) the author wanted to provoke.

The earliest anime I watched was Dragon Ball Z and as a kid seeing super humans go around fighting bad guys and going to all sorts of places was always a fun watch. I got really into Dragon Ball Z, Yu-Gi-Oh, Pokemon, Bo-bo-bo, and various shows that used to play on Cartoon Network in the 2000s. I played the games on my consoles, played fan games on the internet, and really connected with the worlds and story telling. At the time I didn’t know where any of this was from, it was just fun tv shows and cartoons to me.

As time went on and I learned where they came from, how they were made, and seeing Japanese original versions of the shows (usually the US releases were censored compared to the Japanese versions), and so got more into learning more about them. I found out that the “Saiyans” from Dragon Ball were called “Saiyan Jin” in Japanese, later finding out that “Jin” meant “person or people” so literally “the saiyan people or race”. I was fascinated in how the language worked and how it was different than English. I tried to learn by myself at 10 to learning the basics of Japanese but knowing typical 10 year olds I found it hard and gave up. But it didn’t stop my fascination about learning about anime.

In middle school I started watching more shows and was probably the time I watched the most in my life. I went through the backlog of older shows I didn’t watch as a kid, new shows I found online, and some by accident through YouTube videos. I watched action shows, adventure shows, romance, anything you could name (except horror, I still don’t like horror). One common setting however in Japan that seemed to come up the most was Japanese school and cities. Obviously since most of the audience that watches anime in Japan are teens to early adults these are familiar sights to them. But to me I was interested in how different and similar Japan was like.

As I transition to high school I continued watching anime, less so than before but got into more emotionally deep and comedic shows/movies. Some notable examples being the Ghibli movies like before, Clannad (After Story), Kill La Kill, Neon Genesis Evangelion, and Cowboy Bebop. These shows weren’t like the ones I watched as a kid (other than Dragon Ball Z) since it wasn’t just fun action and comedy. They put characters in situations that made them emotionally vulnerable and shows either how they deal with their emotions positively/successfully or not.

Evangelion being famous for this because it’s director in the original creation of the TV show in the 1990s was in a serious episode of depression during it’s creation. Manifesting itself in the main character’s situation and attitude. The character show feelings of despair, hopelessness, and indifference. This show to me is important since it help me understand some emotions I had felt in the past and that other people go through similar emotions in life. I understood feelings of depression weren’t unique to me, but seeing that show showed me how it manifests in others and how despite being made in a country across the globe from me how everyone in life struggles.

After high school and into college I more or less fell off with anime TV shows as a whole, not because I stopped liking them but felt it too time consuming. So I started watching more anime movies and these continued the interest in emotionally heavy media. Newer movies that I watched at that time were:

  • Josee the Tiger and the Fish : Is about a university student who ends up saving and becoming a caretaker for a paraplegic girl, helping her open herself more to the world despite hardships she had in life because of her disability.
  • The Garden of Words : Is about a 15 year old hobbyist shoemaker who meets a 27 year old woman that avoids work due to personal problems at a garden. The hobbyist talks about his interests while the woman keeps to mostly to herself. The message in the movie describing how despite the difference in age the 15 year old is more mature than the 27 year old, showing that age doesn’t automatically equate to maturity.
  • Wolf Children : Is about a college student girl that falls in love with a mysterious man who can transform himself into a wolf. They have children and one day the father dies in an accident, so the mother has to raise her two half wolf half human children in a world that doesn’t understand them.

I noticed over time that I prefer these types of media over more action packed showed I watched in the past and feel more in love with the genres. After I graduated and moved out I found more inspiring and deep movies to which I still hold to my heart even now.

In This Corner of the World

Which takes place in 1930s-1940s Japan, focusing on the main character “Suzu” and her life during that period. She marries a childhood friend and moves to Kura from her home town Hiroshima to live with her husband’s family. She has to live in the harsh time of war where food is rationed. She takes care of the duties around the house and helps take care of her niece. As time goes on the effects of war take their toll and after the nuclear bombing of her hometown she tries to live her life as best she can with the trauma of war.

Maquia: When the Promised Flower Blooms

Takes place in a fictional world where the main character is young girl among the race of Iorph who lived far away from humans and live for hundreds of years. They are not allowed to speak with outsiders and encouraged not to get attached to them.

However an invading kingdom forcefully kills and takes some of the townspeople away, the main character Maquia in an attempt to escapes finds herself holding on to a tangled fabric on an invading dragon flying away. She is sent flying into a forest where she wanders alone lost until she comes across an newborn baby being held tightly by his dead mother. Overcome with grief but not wanting to let the child die she takes him into her care. Although having nowhere to go she wanders aimlessly until she comes across a local woman how has children and helps her until she can get off her feet.

The story continues following the life of Maquia and her adopted son, as she tries so give him the best life. Trying to fight back her trauma of her village and the idea of never getting attached with the outside world. On top of the realization that her son is not of her own race, knowing that she will outlive him.

The last movie having the biggest impact on me, since I felt so invested in the character and her struggles. It helped me understand the struggles of not only single parents but adoptee parents and children. Although not in such extreme examples of the movie, the same emotions of taking care of a child, watching it grow, and going through struggles to provide for the child. As someone who has single parents, this movie hit especially hard. I never cried so much to media ever since Clannad After Story and Interstellar.

And that is the end of where anime impacts my life, the next section being video games and concluding with general Japanese culture.
